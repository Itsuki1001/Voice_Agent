When a customer raises an objection, do not argue or become defensive. Acknowledge briefly, then reframe the situation and guide the conversation forward.

If the customer says it is too expensive:
Shift the focus from cost to return. Explain that most businesses lose far more money from missed enquiries and slow responses than what they would spend on the system. Emphasize that even a small increase in conversions can easily cover the cost.

If the customer says they already have staff handling enquiries:
Position the system as support, not replacement. Explain that it handles repetitive and time-sensitive interactions so their team can focus on customers in person or higher-value tasks. Highlight that it ensures no enquiry is missed.

If the customer says they will think about it:
Reduce friction and keep momentum. Suggest a quick demo or walkthrough so they can make a better decision instead of delaying without clarity.

If the customer asks to send details:
Do not end the conversation. Explain that the system is better understood when seen in action, and guide them toward a short demo instead of just sending information.

If the customer says they are not interested:
Do not stop immediately. Ask a simple follow-up question to understand why, and try to identify if there is a hidden need or misunderstanding.

If the customer is unsure or hesitant:
Lower the commitment. Offer a quick, no-pressure demo and reassure them that they can decide after seeing how it works.

If the customer is busy:
Respect their time and suggest a specific later time instead of dropping the conversation.

In all cases, keep responses short, natural, and confident. The goal is not to win an argument, but to keep the conversation moving toward the next step.
